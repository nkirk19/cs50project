Observations and Conclusions

With each pair of values among unemployment percentage, poverty percentage, education percentage (bachelor or higher), Democrat voting percentage (for the 2016 presidential election), and Republican voting percentage (for the 2016 presidential election), we observed the following correlations—visualized as both correlation plots and a heatmap of correlation strengths:




Education

From the choropleth maps, it can be observed that there are certain areas within the United States where there is a dense population of individuals with a bachelor’s degree or higher. For example, the Northeast is densely populated with those who have attained higher levels of education, likely because of the wealth accumulation, elite universities, and major cities — such as New York, Boston, Providence, and Philadelphia. Moreover, education levels seem to be higher in cities. This is likely because cities host more companies and job opportunities. Lastly, areas that host universities and students are also shown to have higher levels of education.


On a county-by-county basis, there exists a strong negative correlation between having a higher levels of education (bachelor’s degree or higher) and poverty (-48%). This matched our hypothesis prior to the project. While slightly weaker, there is also a negative correlation between having a bachelor’s degree or higher and unemployment (-35%). Similarly, we were not surprised by this fact.


Counties which had a comparatively higher level of education as measured by the bachelors degree or higher percentage correlated relatively strongly (43%) with voting Democratic in the 2016 Presidential Election, while the inverse was true for the correlation between and education level measured by a bachelors degree or higher percentage with voting Republican (-48%).


Unemployment

When observing the choropleth maps, there were certain areas throughout the country that had concentrated levels of poverty. These include areas along the Southern border and Southern states — such as Kentucky, West Virginia, Alabama. On the other hand, New England states have significantly less unemployment rates. To our surprise, states more central in the country — such as Nebraska and Kansas — also had lower levels of unemployment. This might be because the population of these states are relatively low.


In terms of correlation, unemployment unsurprisingly strongly correlates to poverty (55%). As previously mentioned, the correlation between unemployment and education is negative. Both of these correlations were expected.


Unemployment positively correlates with a preference for voting Democrat (20%). Inversely, unemployment correlates negatively with a preference for voting Republican (-16%). While this was expected, we were expecting the correlation values to be higher.


Poverty

The choropleth maps show that poverty rates concentrate in Southern states. This includes high levels of poverty in certain areas in Arizona, New Mexico, Southern Texas, Louisiana, Arkansas, Mississippi, Alabama, Georgia, South Carolina, North Carolina, Kentucky, and West Virginia. This trend is also true for certain areas, like Montana and South Dakota.


Poverty is positively correlated with unemployment and negatively correlated with having a bachelor’s degree or higher. While poverty and employment appear to overlap strongly in a number of regions - such as Eastern Kentucky, New Mexico, Western Mississippi — it is not always the case in other areas. For example, poverty appears to be comparatively higher in some regions of the South East, whereas the unemployment rates in some of those same areas are lower. This may mean that, while individuals are employed and actively working, they may be unable to afford everyday necessities and would thus drop below the federal poverty line.


With regards to party preference, poverty is positively correlated with a preference for voting Democrat (16%) and negatively correlated with a preference for voting Republican (-9%).


Party Preference

While the correlations for the preference of party with education, unemployment, and poverty have been detailed above, the geographic distribution of voters is important to note. From the 2016 presidential election, the majority of U.S. from a geographical coverage percentage voted Republican. In other words, rural areas covering much of the country comparatively voted Republican, whereas strong Democratic support was in the the more densely populated locations within the country.
